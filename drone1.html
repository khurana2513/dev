01Foundation

The Physics
of Drone Flight

Before you touch a motor or a soldering iron, understand WHY a drone flies. Every design decision traces back to these four forces and Newton's laws.

Four Forces Acting on Every Drone

How a Quadcopter Controls Movement

A quadcopter has no moving control surfaces like a helicopter's swashplate. All control comes from differentially adjusting motor speeds. This is the genius of the design — simple, reliable, and fully controllable.

QUADCOPTER MOTOR LAYOUT
  Standard X-frame layout (viewed from above):

        M1 (CCW) ●─────────────● M2 (CW)
                  \           /
                   \   BODY  /
                    ╔═══════╗
                    ║  FC   ║   FC = Flight Controller
                    ╚═══════╝
                   /         \
                  /           \
        M3 (CW)  ●─────────────● M4 (CCW)

  Motor rotation:  CW = Clockwise   CCW = Counter-Clockwise

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  MOVEMENT          MOTOR SPEED CHANGE              RESULT
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  HOVER             M1=M2=M3=M4 (equal)             Stationary
  THROTTLE UP       All motors increase             Climb
  ROLL RIGHT        M1,M3 increase / M2,M4 decrease Tilt right → move right
  PITCH FORWARD     M1,M2 increase / M3,M4 decrease Tilt forward → move forward
  YAW LEFT          M1,M4 increase / M2,M3 decrease Rotate left (torque reaction)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHY YAW WORKS: CW props produce CW torque. CCW props produce CCW torque.
  Increase CW motors → net CW torque → body spins CCW (yaw left).

The Thrust Equation — Understanding Motor Power

T = Ct × ρ × n² × D⁴

T = Thrust [Newtons]
Ct = Thrust coefficient (depends on prop design, typically 0.05–0.15)
ρ = Air density [1.225 kg/m³ at sea level]
n = Rotational speed [revolutions per second]
D = Propeller diameter [meters]

Key insight: Thrust scales with D⁴ — doubling prop diameter gives 16× more thrust potential.
This is why large slow props are more efficient than small fast ones.

Thrust-to-Weight Ratio (TWR) — The Most Important Number

⚡ Critical Concept

TWR = Total Thrust / All-Up Weight. For stable flight you need TWR > 1. For FPV racing: TWR 8–15. For photography: TWR 2.5–4. For efficiency: TWR 1.5–2.5. A drone with TWR = 2 means you have 50% throttle headroom at hover — enough for wind gusts and maneuvers.

TWR = (Motor Count × Max Motor Thrust) / (All-Up Weight × 9.81)

Example: 4 motors × 800g thrust = 3200g total thrust
All-Up Weight = 600g (drone + battery + camera)
TWR = 3200g / 600g = 5.3 — excellent for freestyle FPV

Newton's Laws Applied to Drones

①

First Law — Inertia

A drone in hover stays in hover unless a force acts on it. Wind, control input, or gravity change disturb this. The flight controller continuously counteracts these disturbances.

②

Second Law — F = ma

Net force = mass × acceleration. More thrust → faster climb. Heavier drone → more thrust needed for same acceleration. This governs every performance calculation.

③

Third Law — Reaction

Props push air down (action) → air pushes drone up (reaction). Motors spin CW → torque reaction on body. This is why opposite motor pairs rotate in opposite directions.

02Propulsion

Brushless Motors
— Complete Guide

The heart of every drone. Understanding motor specs isn't optional — the wrong motor choice is the difference between a 20-minute flight and a 6-minute disaster.

Brushed vs Brushless — Why Brushless Wins

✗ Brushed Motors (Avoid)
  • Physical brushes create friction and wear
  • Short lifespan (50–200 hours)
  • Low efficiency (~65–75%)
  • Heat buildup limits sustained performance
  • Only in tiny indoor micros
    • ✓ Brushless Motors (Always)
  • No brushes — electronically commutated
  • Very long lifespan (1000+ hours)
  • High efficiency (~85–95%)
  • Higher power density, less heat
  • Used in everything from 65mm to 30"+ drones

    Propellers —
    Geometry & Selection

    Propellers are the most underrated component. The same motor behaves completely differently with different props. Choose wrong and you waste power, shorten battery life, and get poor performance.

    Decoding Prop Notation — 5x4.3x3

    PROP SPECIFICATION
      5 × 4.3 × 3
  │     │     └── Number of blades (2, 3, or 4)
  │     └──────── Pitch: inches of forward travel per revolution
  └────────────── Diameter: inches (tip to tip)

  DIAMETER governs thrust and efficiency.
  PITCH governs top speed and current draw.
  BLADES governs thrust density (more blades = more thrust, less efficient).

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  SAME DIAMETER, HIGHER PITCH:                                │
  │  → More aggressive, higher top speed, more current draw      │
  │  → Better for racing, worse for efficiency and hover time    │
  │                                                              │
  │  SAME PITCH, LARGER DIAMETER:                                │
  │  → More thrust, more efficient, lower RPM needed             │
  │  → Better for photography/LR, limited by frame size          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Blade Count Trade-offs

    ✌

    2-Blade

    Most efficient. Best flight time. Lower thrust density. Common on long-range and 7"+ builds.

    🤟

    3-Blade

    Best balance. Standard for FPV. Better response than 2-blade. Slightly less efficient. Default choice for 5" freestyle.

    🖐

    4-Blade

    More thrust in smaller diameter. Better handling at low throttle. Popular on 5" racing builds. Lower efficiency.

    🖐✌

    5–6 Blade

    Niche. Used on coaxial designs or cinewhoops needing max thrust with prop guards. Inefficient, noisy.

    04Power System

    LiPo Batteries &
    Power System

    The battery defines everything — flight time, power delivery, weight. Getting this wrong means either dead batteries after 3 minutes or a battery that overheats and catches fire.

    LiPo Chemistry — Why LiPo?

    ⚡

    Energy Density

    150–200 Wh/kg vs 50–80 Wh/kg for NiMH. LiPo gives 2–3× more energy for the same weight — essential for drones.

    💨

    High Discharge

    Can deliver 75–150C burst current — enough for motors demanding 40–200A peaks. Lead-acid or NiMH can't match this.

    🔥

    But: Dangerous

    Overcharge, puncture, or short-circuit = thermal runaway = fire. LiPo fires are fast, hot (800°C), and produce toxic gas. Respect these batteries.

    Decoding Battery Specs — 4S 1500mAh 75C

    BATTERY NOMENCLATURE
      4S  1500mAh  75C

  4S = 4 cells in Series
       Each cell = 3.7V nominal (4.2V full, 3.0V cutoff)
       4S voltage: 14.8V nominal, 16.8V full, 12.0V cutoff

  ┌─────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  1S │ 3.7V │ Tiny whoops, mini quads, FPV goggles         │
  │  2S │ 7.4V │ Micro drones, some long range                │
  │  3S │11.1V │ 3" micros, some 5" builds                    │
  │  4S │14.8V │ Standard 5" FPV — most common                │
  │  5S │18.5V │ High performance 5", long range 7"           │
  │  6S │22.2V │ 5" efficiency builds, 7–10" photography      │
  └─────┴─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  1500mAh = Capacity (how much charge stored)
           = 1.5Ah → at 1A draw, runs for 1.5 hours

  75C = Continuous discharge rate
       Max continuous current = 75 × 1.5 = 112.5A
       Some packs have burst C-rating (e.g. 75C/150C)

    C-Rating Reality Check

    ⚠ Industry Truth

    C-ratings on budget batteries are often grossly exaggerated. A battery claiming 100C may only deliver 40–50C reliably. The real measure is internal resistance (IR). Lower IR = better pack. A fresh 4S pack should have <5 milliohms total IR. Above 20 milliohms = pack is dying. Use a battery checker with IR measurement (iSDT, Junsi).

    LiPo Safety — Non-Negotiable Rules

    1. Never charge above 4.2V per cell (4.35V for HV LiHV). Overcharge causes thermal runaway. Use a balance charger that checks each cell individually.
    2. Never discharge below 3.0V per cell under load (3.5V safe minimum for longevity). Overdischarge permanently damages the pack — cells swell and capacity drops 30–50%.
    3. Store at 3.8V per cell (storage charge). Leaving a pack fully charged or fully discharged for days damages it. After every session: storage charge.
    4. Use a LiPo-safe charging bag or ammo box when charging unattended. Fires start fast and spread faster.
    5. Inspect for puffing before every flight. A swollen pack has gas inside from internal damage — DO NOT fly it. Dispose of correctly (salt water discharge).
    6. Never charge a warm battery. Let packs cool to room temp after flying. Hot + charging = accelerated degradation or fire.

    05Control

    Electronic Speed
    Controllers (ESC)

    The ESC is the translator between the flight controller's intent and the motor's action — converting DC battery power into precisely timed 3-phase AC for brushless motors at thousands of times per second.

    How an ESC Works

    ESC SIGNAL FLOW
      Flight Controller          ESC                    Motor
  ┌──────────────┐    ┌──────────────────┐    ┌──────────┐
  │              │    │  MCU (STM32F0/   │    │          │
  │  Throttle %  │───►│  AT32)           │    │  A  B  C │
  │  (0–100%)    │    │  ↓               │    │  │  │  │ │
  │              │    │  MOSFET Driver   │───►│ Phases   │
  │  DSHOT600    │    │  ↓               │    │          │
  │  signal      │    │  6× MOSFETs      │    │  Spins!  │
  └──────────────┘    │  (3 high + 3 low)│    └──────────┘
                      └──────────────────┘

  MOSFET switching sequence creates 3-phase AC from DC battery.
  Switching happens at 8,000–48,000 times/second (PWM frequency).

    OneShot125Analog~2.5msNo5× faster than PWM. Legacy.

    4-in-1 vs Individual ESCs

    ✓ 4-in-1 ESC (Recommended)
  • One board, clean build
  • Integrated current sensor
  • Shared ground = less noise
  • Often includes BEC for 5V/9V/12V
  • Easier wiring, less weight
  • Easier to replace: one swap for all
    • ✗ Individual ESCs
  • Messier wiring
  • More weight and space used
  • Need separate PDB
  • But: replace one without replacing all
  • Can use longer motor wires (better cooling)
  • Common on larger photography drones

    • ESC Firmware — BLHeli_32 vs AM32

    ℹ Firmware Matters

    BLHeli_32 — Industry standard, closed source, excellent performance. Supports DSHOT600, bidirectional DSHOT, RPM filtering, 3D mode. Most quality ESCs ship with this. AM32 — Open source alternative, actively developed, same features, growing support. Both are excellent — BLHeli_32 is more universal. Avoid old BLHeli_S (8-bit, limited) unless on a budget micro build.

    ESC Current Rating — How to Choose

    Required ESC current = Max motor current × 1.25 (safety margin)

    Example: Motor draws 45A max
    Required ESC: 45 × 1.25 = 56.25A → Use 60A ESC minimum

    For 4-in-1: rating is per motor channel.
    A "60A 4-in-1" handles 60A per corner, not 60A total.

    The PID Control Loop — How Stability Works

    PID CONTROL LOOP
      Pilot stick input → Target angle/rate
          │
          ▼
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                 PID CONTROLLER                    │
  │                                                   │
  │  Error = Target − Actual (from gyro)              │
  │                                                   │
  │  P (Proportional): Output = P × Error             │
  │  → Corrects current error. Too high = oscillation │
  │                                                   │
  │  I (Integral): Output = I × ∫Error dt             │
  │  → Corrects accumulated drift. Too high = wind-up │
  │                                                   │
  │  D (Derivative): Output = D × dError/dt           │
  │  → Predicts future error, dampens oscillations    │
  │  → Sensitive to noise — requires filtering        │
  │                                                   │
  │  Total = P + I + D → Motor correction signal      │
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
          │
          ▼
  ESC → Motor speed change → Drone corrects
          │
          ▼

    Failsafe — Critical Configuration

    🚨 NEVER Skip Failsafe Setup

    Failsafe defines what the drone does when it loses RC signal. Options: Disarm (motors stop — drone falls), Hold (hover in place), RTH/Return to Home (GPS required). For FPV without GPS: set failsafe to DISARM. For GPS-equipped: set to RTH. Test failsafe by turning off your radio at low altitude over soft ground BEFORE every new build flies for the first time.

    VTX Power Selection

    ⚠ Legal & Practical Note

    VTX power is regulated. In most countries, 25mW is the legal limit without license. India: use <25mW or obtain an amateur radio license (5.8GHz is in unlicensed bands — but check TRAI/WPC rules). For local flying: 25–200mW. For long range outdoors: 400–800mW with proper antenna setups. Always use SmartAudio/IRC Tramp to control VTX power from your radio so you can set 25mW in controlled areas without opening the drone.

    Magnetometer Interference — Critical Setup Note

    🚨 Compass Calibration is Life-Critical

    The compass (magnetometer) tells the drone which way is North — essential for GPS modes. It is extremely sensitive to interference from power cables, ESCs, and motors. Mount GPS/compass on a mast at least 10cm above the frame. Never run power wires near it. Calibrate compass after every significant build change. Compass errors cause toilet bowling (spinning around home point) and flyaways.

    ND Filters — Why They Matter for Aerial Video

    ℹ 180° Shutter Rule

    For cinematic motion blur, shutter speed should be 2× frame rate (180° rule). At 30fps, use 1/60s. At 60fps, use 1/120s. On a bright day, this requires ND filters to avoid overexposure. ND4 = 2 stops. ND8 = 3 stops. ND16 = 4 stops. ND32 = 5 stops. Use Polar Pro or Freewell filter sets for your camera. Without NDs, your video looks sharp but uncinematic — stroboscopic and clinical.

    RPM Filtering — The Modern Revolution

    ✓ Enable This on Every Build

    RPM filtering uses real-time motor RPM data (via Bidirectional DSHOT) to create dynamic notch filters exactly at motor and prop noise frequencies. This eliminates the need for heavy low-pass filtering that adds latency. Result: cleaner D-term, less heat, better flight feel, cleaner video. Enable in Betaflight: Filtering tab → RPM filter ON. Set bidir_dshot = ON in CLI. This single feature improved FPV drone performance more than any hardware change in years.

    Wiring Order — 5" Build (Follow Exactly)

    1. Dry fit all components to frame before soldering anything. Identify cable routing, check ESC–FC stack fits, check camera angle.
    2. Solder motors to ESC: any 2 wires swapped = reverse rotation. Do this first while you have space. Use 22AWG silicone wire.
    3. Solder battery leads to 4-in-1 ESC: XT60 connector. Use flux, tin both sides first. DO NOT plug in battery yet. Large copper pads require high-temp iron (380°C).
    4. Solder capacitor (1000µF 35V) across battery pads. Observe polarity. Short lead = negative.
    5. FC–ESC stack: connect via supplied 8-pin cable or direct solder pads. Map UART/signal pads between boards.
    6. Solder VTX: 5V/GND from FC, Video and Audio signals. Install SMA pigtail through frame hole before soldering antenna connector.
    7. Solder camera: 5V/GND, video wire to VTX video input or FC OSD input.
    8. Bind receiver to radio before installing in frame. Test RC input works via Betaflight configurator.
    9. Motor spin direction check: connect to Betaflight, use Motor tab (NO PROPS), verify each motor spins correct direction. Swap any 2 motor wires to reverse.
    10. Betaflight configuration: set DSHOT600, enable RPM filter, assign receiver UART, configure failsafe, set rates. Mandatory before first flight.

    🚨 Never Fly Here — India Red Zones

    Within 5km of any airport. Within 25km of international borders. Over military installations. Over the President's Estate, Parliament, Supreme Court, State Legislatures. Over critical infrastructure (power plants, refineries). At night (unless BVLOS permit). Over crowds. Violating these rules can result in imprisonment up to 1 year and fines up to ₹10 lakh under DGCA regulations and IT Act sections.

    Digital Sky Portal — How to Register

    1. Go to digitalsky.dgca.gov.in — the official DGCA portal for drone registration and permissions.
    2. Register as a Drone Operator. Upload ID proof, address, and drone details (serial number, manufacturer, max MTOW).
    3. Drone receives a UIN (Unique Identification Number) — must be displayed on the drone.
    4. Apply for UAOP (Unmanned Aircraft Operator Permit) for micro+ drones before flying.
    5. For each flight in yellow zones: file a flight permission request via Digital Sky, minimum 24 hours before.
    6. For FPV racing in a club: clubs can apply for a standing permission in a designated area.

    Blackbox — Your Most Powerful Tuning Tool

    ⚡ Pro Tip — Always Use Blackbox

    Betaflight Blackbox records gyro data, motor outputs, RC inputs, and PID corrections at 2–8kHz to an onboard SD card or SPI flash. Analyze in Betaflight Blackbox Explorer or PIDtoolbox. You can literally see oscillations in the data, find prop resonance frequencies, validate filter effectiveness, and tune PIDs without guessing. No serious tuner makes changes without Blackbox data. If your FC has an SD card slot — use it.
